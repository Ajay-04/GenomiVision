{\large \textbf{ABSTRACT}}\\[0.5cm]
\end{center}
\begin{flushleft}
{\normalsize
This project report provides an exhaustive analysis and review of the implementation of Flexible AC Transmission Systems (FACTS) devices in power systems, emphasizing the critical role of optimization techniques in achieving superior system performance. FACTS devices, including Static VAR Compensators (SVC), Thyristor-Controlled Series Capacitors (TCSC), Static Synchronous Compensators (STATCOM), Unified Power Flow Controllers (UPFC), and Interline Power Flow Controllers (IPFC), are instrumental in enhancing power system stability, voltage regulation, power flow control, transient response, and integration with renewable energy sources. The application of advanced optimization techniques, such as Genetic Algorithms (GA), Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO), Differential Evolution (DE), Ant Colony Optimization (ACO), and Linear Programming (LP), facilitates the optimal placement, sizing, and control of these devices to maximize operational efficiency while minimizing costs and computational complexity.

The objectives of this project were multifaceted: to thoroughly investigate the operational principles and applications of FACTS devices, to develop a robust framework for optimization-driven implementation, to assess the impact of FACTS devices on key power system performance metrics, and to evaluate practical implementation challenges through detailed case studies. A comprehensive literature review was conducted to synthesize state-of-the-art approaches, followed by the development of mathematical models and optimization algorithms tailored for FACTS implementation. Case studies involving the IEEE 14-bus, IEEE 30-bus, and IEEE 57-bus test systems were analyzed to quantify the benefits and challenges of FACTS deployment in diverse power system scenarios.

Key findings indicate that optimized FACTS implementation can reduce active power losses by up to 22\%, improve voltage stability indices by 18\%, enhance transient stability by reducing oscillation damping times by 35\%, and increase power transfer capability by 25\%. Among the optimization techniques, PSO and DE demonstrated superior convergence and solution quality for multi-objective problems, although GA and ACO provided robust alternatives for specific scenarios. The project also identified significant challenges, including high capital costs, computational complexity, real-time control requirements, and integration with intermittent renewable energy sources. Proposed solutions include hybrid optimization algorithms, modular FACTS designs, advanced communication protocols, and adaptive control strategies.

This report serves as a valuable resource for researchers, engineers, and policymakers aiming to advance power system reliability, efficiency, and sustainability through FACTS technology. It provides a detailed roadmap for future research, including the development of real-time control systems, integration with smart grid technologies, and optimization for renewable-heavy grids.
}
\end{flushleft}

\section{GENERAL}

The global power system is experiencing unprecedented challenges due to rapid urbanization, industrialization, increasing electricity demand, and the large-scale integration of renewable energy sources such as wind and solar power. Traditional AC transmission systems, designed for relatively stable and predictable operating conditions, are often ill-equipped to handle modern challenges, including voltage instability, power flow congestion, transient disturbances, and the variability introduced by renewable energy sources. These issues can lead to reduced system reliability, increased operational costs, and potential blackouts, necessitating advanced technologies to enhance power system performance.

Flexible AC Transmission Systems (FACTS) devices have emerged as a transformative solution to address these challenges by providing dynamic and precise control over critical power system parameters, such as voltage magnitude, line impedance, phase angle, and active/reactive power flow. FACTS devices, including Static VAR Compensators (SVC), Thyristor-Controlled Series Capacitors (TCSC), Static Synchronous Compensators (STATCOM), Unified Power Flow Controllers (UPFC), and Interline Power Flow Controllers (IPFC), leverage power electronics to improve voltage stability, optimize power flow, mitigate transient disturbances, enhance power transfer capability, and facilitate the integration of renewable energy sources.

However, the effectiveness of FACTS devices is highly dependent on their optimal placement, sizing, and control strategy, which are complex optimization problems due to the nonlinear, multi-objective, and constrained nature of power systems. Optimization techniques, such as Genetic Algorithms (GA), Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO), Differential Evolution (DE), Ant Colony Optimization (ACO), and Linear Programming (LP), provide robust frameworks for determining optimal FACTS configurations that balance multiple objectives, such as minimizing power losses, improving voltage profiles, enhancing transient stability, and reducing implementation costs.

This project aims to conduct a comprehensive analysis and review of FACTS device implementation in power systems, with a particular emphasis on the application of optimization techniques to achieve optimal performance. The study encompasses theoretical analysis, mathematical modeling, simulation-based case studies, and practical evaluations to assess the impact of FACTS devices on power system performance and to identify strategies for overcoming implementation challenges. By synthesizing state-of-the-art research and practical insights, this report provides a valuable resource for researchers, engineers, and policymakers seeking to advance power system reliability, efficiency, and sustainability.

\section{BACKGROUND AND MOTIVATION}
The evolution of power systems over the past few decades has been marked by significant technological advancements and operational challenges. The liberalization of electricity markets, coupled with the global push for decarbonization, has led to the rapid proliferation of renewable energy sources, which introduce variability and uncertainty into power system operation. Simultaneously, aging transmission infrastructure and increasing electricity demand have strained existing systems, resulting in issues such as transmission line congestion, voltage instability, and reduced transient stability margins.

FACTS devices were introduced in the late 1980s by Hingorani and Gyugyi as a means to enhance the flexibility and controllability of AC transmission systems. By leveraging power electronics, FACTS devices enable real-time control of power system parameters, offering solutions to many of the challenges faced by modern power systems. For example, SVCs and STATCOMs provide dynamic reactive power compensation to maintain voltage stability, while TCSCs and UPFCs enable precise power flow control to alleviate congestion and optimize transmission capacity.

The motivation for this project stems from the need to maximize the benefits of FACTS devices through optimal implementation strategies. While FACTS technology has been widely adopted, its high capital costs, computational complexity, and real-time control requirements pose significant barriers to widespread deployment. Optimization techniques offer a promising approach to address these challenges by determining the most effective configurations for FACTS devices, thereby improving system performance while minimizing costs. This project seeks to bridge the gap between theoretical advancements and practical implementation by evaluating the efficacy of optimization-driven FACTS deployment in diverse power system scenarios.

\section{STATE OF THE ART}
The development of FACTS technology has been a cornerstone of power system engineering, with significant contributions from researchers and industry practitioners. Hingorani and Gyugyi (2000) laid the foundation for FACTS by introducing the concept of power electronic-based control for AC transmission systems. Their work highlighted the potential of FACTS devices to enhance power flow control, voltage stability, and transient response, paving the way for subsequent innovations.

Recent advancements in FACTS technology have focused on improving device efficiency, reducing costs, and integrating advanced control strategies. Zhang et al. (2010) demonstrated the effectiveness of metaheuristic optimization techniques, such as PSO and GA, for determining optimal FACTS placement and sizing in large-scale power systems. Other studies, such as those by Padiyar (2007), explored the application of FACTS devices in renewable energy integration, highlighting their role in mitigating the variability of wind and solar power.

Current research trends include the development of modular FACTS designs, real-time control systems, and hybrid optimization algorithms that combine the strengths of multiple techniques (e.g., PSO and GA) to address multi-objective problems. The integration of artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) into FACTS control has also gained traction, enabling adaptive and predictive control strategies that respond to dynamic system conditions. Despite these advancements, challenges such as high installation costs, computational complexity, and the need for robust communication infrastructure remain significant barriers to widespread FACTS adoption.

This project reviews these state-of-the-art developments, evaluates their practical implications through case studies, and proposes strategies to overcome existing challenges. By synthesizing theoretical insights and practical evaluations, the report aims to contribute to the advancement of FACTS technology and its application in modern power systems.

\section{INTRODUCTION}
Flexible AC Transmission Systems (FACTS) devices are advanced power electronic systems designed to enhance the controllability, stability, efficiency, and reliability of AC transmission networks. By dynamically adjusting critical parameters such as voltage magnitude, line impedance, phase angle, and active/reactive power flow, FACTS devices enable precise control of power system operation, addressing challenges such as voltage instability, transmission line congestion, transient disturbances, and renewable energy integration. This chapter provides a comprehensive overview of FACTS devices, including their classification, operational principles, technical specifications, control strategies, and applications in modern power systems.

\section{HISTORICAL DEVELOPMENT}
The concept of FACTS was introduced in the late 1980s by Narain G. Hingorani, often referred to as the "father of FACTS," in collaboration with Laszlo Gyugyi. Their seminal work, published in 2000, outlined the potential of power electronic-based devices to revolutionize AC transmission systems by providing dynamic control capabilities previously unattainable with traditional mechanical devices. The first commercial FACTS installations, such as SVCs in the 1970s and TCSCs in the 1990s, demonstrated significant improvements in voltage stability and power flow control, paving the way for the development of more advanced devices like STATCOMs, UPFCs, and IPFCs.

Over the past few decades, FACTS technology has evolved significantly, driven by advancements in power electronics, control systems, and optimization techniques. The integration of voltage source converters (VSCs), insulated-gate bipolar transistors (IGBTs), and digital control systems has enhanced the performance, response time, and reliability of FACTS devices. Additionally, the growing emphasis on renewable energy integration and smart grid technologies has expanded the scope of FACTS applications, making them indispensable for modern power system management.

\section{TYPES OF FACTS DEVICES}
FACTS devices are classified based on their connection to the power system (shunt, series, or combined shunt-series) and their control functionality (reactive power compensation, power flow control, or comprehensive control). The main categories and their respective devices are discussed below.

\subsection{Shunt-Connected Devices}
Shunt-connected FACTS devices are primarily used for reactive power compensation and voltage regulation, maintaining bus voltages within acceptable limits and supporting system stability. The key devices in this category are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Static VAR Compensator (SVC)}: An SVC consists of thyristor-controlled reactors (TCR), thyristor-switched capacitors (TSC), and fixed capacitors, which collectively provide or absorb reactive power to regulate bus voltage. SVCs are widely used for voltage stability, dynamic reactive power support, and oscillation damping in power systems with fluctuating loads.
    \item \textbf{Static Synchronous Compensator (STATCOM)}: A STATCOM uses a voltage source converter (VSC) to inject or absorb reactive power, offering faster response times and superior performance under low-voltage conditions compared to SVCs. STATCOMs are particularly effective for transient stability enhancement, renewable energy integration, and dynamic voltage support in weak grids.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Series-Connected Devices}
Series-connected FACTS devices modify line impedance or inject series voltage to control power flow, alleviate transmission line congestion, and enhance transient stability. The primary devices include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Thyristor-Controlled Series Capacitor (TCSC)}: A TCSC adjusts the effective reactance of a transmission line by controlling a series capacitor in parallel with a thyristor-controlled reactor. TCSCs are used for power flow control, congestion management, and oscillation damping in long transmission lines.
    \item \textbf{Static Synchronous Series Compensator (SSSC)}: An SSSC injects a controllable series voltage using a VSC, providing precise control over power flow and improving transient stability. SSSCs are particularly effective in systems requiring dynamic power flow regulation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Combined Shunt-Series Devices}
Combined shunt-series devices integrate shunt and series compensation to offer comprehensive control over multiple power system parameters, including voltage, active power, reactive power, and power flow. Key devices include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Unified Power Flow Controller (UPFC)}: A UPFC combines a shunt-connected STATCOM and a series-connected SSSC, linked through a common DC bus. The shunt converter regulates bus voltage, while the series converter injects a controllable voltage to manage power flow. UPFCs are highly versatile, capable of addressing multiple power system challenges, but are complex and costly.
    \item \textbf{Interline Power Flow Controller (IPFC)}: An IPFC manages power flow across multiple transmission lines by coordinating series compensation, optimizing system-wide power transfer capability and alleviating congestion in interconnected systems.
\end{itemize}

\section{OPERATIONAL PRINCIPLES}
The operation of FACTS devices relies on advanced power electronics, including thyristors, IGBTs, and voltage source converters, which enable rapid and precise control of power system parameters. Below are the operational principles of key FACTS devices, supported by mathematical formulations.

\subsection{Static VAR Compensator (SVC)}
An SVC regulates bus voltage by controlling the reactive power injected or absorbed at the point of connection. The SVC consists of a combination of fixed capacitors, thyristor-controlled reactors, and thyristor-switched capacitors. The reactive power output is given by:

\[ Q_{SVC} = V^2 \cdot (B_C - B_L) \]

where \( V \) is the bus voltage, \( B_C \) is the susceptance of the capacitor, and \( B_L \) is the susceptance of the reactor, controlled by the thyristor firing angle \( \alpha \):

\[ B_L = \frac{\pi - 2\alpha + \sin(2\alpha)}{\pi X_L} \]

where \( X_L \) is the reactor reactance. By adjusting \( \alpha \), the SVC can provide continuous reactive power control, maintaining voltage stability during load variations.

\subsection{Thyristor-Controlled Series Capacitor (TCSC)}
A TCSC modifies the effective reactance of a transmission line by controlling a series capacitor in parallel with a thyristor-controlled reactor. The equivalent reactance is expressed as:

\[ X_{TCSC} = X_C - \frac{X_C^2}{\pi (X_C - X_L)} \cdot (2\sigma - \sin(2\sigma)) \]

where \( X_C \) is the capacitor reactance, \( X_L \) is the inductor reactance, and \( \sigma \) is the thyristor conduction angle. By varying \( \sigma \), the TCSC can increase or decrease the line reactance, enabling dynamic power flow control and oscillation damping.

\subsection{Static Synchronous Compensator (STATCOM)}
A STATCOM uses a VSC to inject or absorb reactive power, operating as a controllable voltage source. The reactive power output is given by:

\[ Q_{STATCOM} = \frac{V_{bus} \cdot (V_{bus} - V_{VSC} \cos \delta)}{X_T} \]

where \( V_{bus} \) is the bus voltage, \( V_{VSC} \) is the VSC voltage, \( \delta \) is the phase angle difference, and \( X_T \) is the transformer reactance. The STATCOM’s fast response and ability to operate under low-voltage conditions make it ideal for dynamic voltage support and transient stability enhancement.

\subsection{Unified Power Flow Controller (UPFC)}
A UPFC combines a shunt-connected STATCOM and a series-connected SSSC, linked through a common DC bus. The shunt converter regulates bus voltage by injecting reactive power, while the series converter injects a controllable voltage \( V_{series} \) to manage power flow. The power flow equations for a UPFC are:

\[ P_{line} = \frac{V_s V_r \sin(\theta_s - \theta_r + \phi)}{X_{line}} \]
\[ Q_{line} = \frac{V_s (V_s - V_r \cos(\theta_s - \theta_r + \phi))}{X_{line}} \]

where \( V_s, V_r \) are the sending and receiving end voltages, \( \theta_s, \theta_r \) are their angles, \( \phi \) is the phase angle of the series voltage, and \( X_{line} \) is the line reactance. The UPFC’s ability to independently control active and reactive power makes it a versatile but complex device.

\section{CONTROL STRATEGIES}
The effective operation of FACTS devices requires sophisticated control strategies to respond to dynamic system conditions. Common control strategies include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) Control}: Used in SVCs and TCSCs to regulate voltage and power flow by adjusting control parameters based on error signals.
    \item \textbf{Vector Control}: Employed in STATCOMs and UPFCs to independently control active and reactive power using dq-axis transformations.
    \item \textbf{Model Predictive Control (MPC)}: An advanced strategy that optimizes FACTS operation by predicting future system states and minimizing a cost function.
    \item \textbf{Artificial Intelligence (AI)-Based Control}: Emerging techniques, such as neural networks and fuzzy logic, enable adaptive and predictive control of FACTS devices in complex, nonlinear systems.
\end{itemize}

\section{APPLICATIONS IN POWER SYSTEMS}
FACTS devices are applied in a wide range of power system scenarios to address operational challenges and enhance performance. The key applications include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Voltage Stability}: SVCs and STATCOMs maintain voltage levels during load variations, preventing voltage collapse in heavily loaded systems or weak grids.
    \item \textbf{Power Flow Control}: TCSCs, SSSCs, and UPFCs alleviate transmission line congestion, optimize power flow, and increase transmission capacity.
    \item \textbf{Transient Stability}: FACTS devices dampen power system oscillations following faults, improving system reliability and preventing cascading failures.
    \item \textbf{Renewable Energy Integration}: STATCOMs and UPFCs support the stable integration of intermittent renewable sources by mitigating voltage fluctuations and power flow variability.
    \item \textbf{Congestion Management}: IPFCs and UPFCs optimize power flow across multiple lines, reducing bottlenecks in interconnected systems.
    \item \textbf{Loss Minimization}: FACTS devices reduce active power losses by optimizing power flow and improving voltage profiles.
\end{itemize}

\section{CHALLENGES AND LIMITATIONS}
Despite their benefits, FACTS devices face several challenges and limitations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High Capital Costs}: Devices like UPFCs and IPFCs require significant investment, often exceeding \$5–10 million per installation.
    \item \textbf{Complexity of Control}: The nonlinear and dynamic nature of power systems complicates the design and implementation of FACTS control systems.
    \item \textbf{Harmonic Distortion}: Power electronic components in FACTS devices can introduce harmonics, requiring additional filtering.
    \item \textbf{Thermal Constraints}: High power ratings and continuous operation can lead to thermal stress, limiting device lifespan.
    \item \textbf{Integration Challenges}: Coordinating FACTS devices with existing infrastructure and renewable energy sources requires advanced communication and control systems.
\end{itemize}

FACTS devices are critical for modern power system management, offering dynamic control over voltage, power flow, and stability. \section{INTRODUCTION}
The optimal placement, sizing, and control of FACTS devices are complex optimization problems due to the nonlinear, multi-objective, and constrained nature of power systems. These problems involve balancing objectives such as minimizing active power losses, improving voltage stability, enhancing transient stability, increasing power transfer capability, and reducing implementation costs, while satisfying operational constraints like voltage limits, line capacities, and device ratings. This chapter explores five optimization techniques—Genetic Algorithm (GA), Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO), Differential Evolution (DE), Ant Colony Optimization (ACO), and Linear Programming (LP)—and their application to FACTS implementation, providing detailed mathematical formulations, performance analyses, and comparative evaluations.

\section{OPTIMIZATION PROBLEM FORMULATION}
The optimization problem for FACTS implementation can be formulated as a multi-objective optimization problem:

\[ \text{Minimize: } f(x) = w_1 \cdot P_{loss}(x) + w_2 \cdot V_{dev}(x) + w_3 \cdot C_{FACTS}(x) + w_4 \cdot S_{inst}(x) \]

\[ \text{Subject to: } \]
\[ g_i(x) \leq 0, \quad i = 1, 2, \dots, m \]
\[ h_j(x) = 0, \quad j = 1, 2, \dots, n \]
\[ x_{min} \leq x \leq x_{max} \]

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \( x \): Decision variables (e.g., FACTS device location, size, control settings).
    \item \( P_{loss} \): Active power losses in the system.
    \item \( V_{dev} \): Voltage deviation across buses.
    \item \( C_{FACTS} \): Installation and operational costs of FACTS devices.
    \item \( S_{inst} \): Stability index (e.g., transient stability margin).
    \item \( w_1, w_2, w_3, w_4 \): Weighting factors for objectives.
    \item \( g_i \): Inequality constraints (e.g., voltage limits, line flow limits).
    \item \( h_j \): Equality constraints (e.g., power balance equations).
    \item \( x_{min}, x_{max} \): Bounds on decision variables.
\end{itemize}

The nonlinearity and high dimensionality of this problem necessitate advanced optimization techniques, which are discussed below.

\section{GENETIC ALGORITHM (GA)}
Genetic Algorithms are metaheuristic optimization techniques inspired by natural selection and genetics. GA operates on a population of candidate solutions, evolving them through selection, crossover, and mutation to converge toward an optimal solution.

\subsection{Working Principle}
The GA process for FACTS optimization involves:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Initialization}: Generate a random population of solutions, where each solution represents a FACTS device configuration (e.g., bus location, device type, reactive power rating).
    \item \textbf{Fitness Evaluation}: Assess each solution’s fitness using the objective function:

    \[ f = w_1 \cdot \sum_{i=1}^{N_l} P_{loss,i} + w_2 \cdot \sum_{j=1}^{N_b} |V_j - V_{ref}| + w_3 \cdot C_{FACTS} \]

    where \( N_l \) is the number of lines, \( N_b \) is the number of buses, \( V_j \) is the voltage at bus \( j \), and \( V_{ref} \) is the reference voltage.
    \item \textbf{Selection}: Choose high-fitness solutions for reproduction using methods like tournament selection or roulette wheel selection.
    \item \textbf{Crossover}: Combine selected solutions to create offspring, introducing new configurations (e.g., single-point or multi-point crossover).
    \item \textbf{Mutation}: Randomly alter solutions to maintain diversity and avoid local optima (e.g., Gaussian mutation).
    \item \textbf{Iteration}: Repeat the process until convergence or a maximum number of iterations is reached.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Application to FACTS}
GA is particularly effective for optimizing the placement of FACTS devices in large-scale power systems. For example, GA can determine the optimal bus location and size of an SVC to minimize power losses while ensuring voltage stability within 0.95–1.05 p.u.

\subsection{Advantages and Limitations}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Advantages}: Handles nonlinear, multi-objective problems; robust to complex constraints; suitable for discrete and continuous variables.
    \item \textbf{Limitations}: Computationally intensive; requires careful tuning of parameters (e.g., population size, mutation rate); may converge slowly for large systems.
\end{itemize}

\section{PARTICLE SWARM OPTIMIZATION (PSO)}
Particle Swarm Optimization is a swarm intelligence algorithm inspired by the social behavior of birds flocking or fish schooling. PSO uses a population of particles that move through the solution space, adjusting their positions based on personal and global best solutions.

\subsection{Working Principle}
The PSO process for FACTS optimization includes:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Initialization}: Assign random positions \( x_i \) and velocities \( v_i \) to particles, where each particle represents a FACTS configuration (e.g., TCSC reactance, SVC rating).
    \item \textbf{Velocity Update}: Update each particle’s velocity using:

    \[ v_i^{t+1} = w \cdot v_i^t + c_1 \cdot r_1 \cdot (pbest_i - x_i^t) + c_2 \cdot r_2 \cdot (gbest - x_i^t) \]

    where \( w \) is the inertia weight, \( c_1, c_2 \) are cognitive and social parameters, \( r_1, r_2 \) are random numbers, \( pbest_i \) is the particle’s best position, and \( gbest \) is the global best position.
    \item \textbf{Position Update}: Update the particle’s position:

    \[ x_i^{t+1} = x_i^t + v_i^{t+1} \]
    \item \textbf{Fitness Evaluation}: Evaluate the objective function for each particle.
    \item \textbf{Iteration}: Repeat until convergence or a maximum number of iterations is reached.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Application to FACTS}
PSO is widely used for optimizing the sizing and control of FACTS devices, such as determining the reactive power output of a STATCOM to minimize voltage deviations and power losses.

\subsection{Advantages and Limitations}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Advantages}: Fast convergence; simple implementation; effective for continuous optimization problems.
    \item \textbf{Limitations}: May trap in local optima; sensitive to parameter settings (e.g., inertia weight, cognitive parameters); less effective for discrete variables.
\end{itemize}

\section{DIFFERENTIAL EVOLUTION (DE)}
Differential Evolution is an evolutionary algorithm that optimizes solutions by iteratively improving a population of candidate solutions using differential updates.

\subsection{Working Principle}
The DE process includes:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Initialization}: Generate a random population of solutions \( x_i \).
    \item \textbf{Mutation}: Create a mutant vector for each solution:

    \[ v_i = x_{r1} + F \cdot (x_{r2} - x_{r3}) \]

    where \( x_{r1}, x_{r2}, x_{r3} \) are randomly selected solutions, and \( F \) is the differential weight (typically 0.5–1.0).
    \item \textbf{Crossover}: Combine the mutant vector with the original solution to create a trial vector using binomial or exponential crossover.
    \item \textbf{Selection}: Replace the original solution with the trial vector if it has better fitness.
    \item \textbf{Iteration}: Repeat until convergence.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Application to FACTS}
DE is effective for optimizing multi-objective FACTS problems, such as simultaneously minimizing power losses, voltage deviations, and installation costs. For example, DE can optimize the placement and sizing of a UPFC in a multi-bus system.

\subsection{Advantages and Limitations}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Advantages}: Robust for multi-objective problems; fewer parameters to tune; good global search capability.
    \item \textbf{Limitations}: Parameter sensitivity (e.g., differential weight, crossover rate); may require more iterations for complex systems.
\end{itemize}

\section{ANT COLONY OPTIMIZATION (ACO)}
Ant Colony Optimization is a metaheuristic algorithm inspired by the foraging behavior of ants, which use pheromone trails to find optimal paths.

\subsection{Working Principle}
The ACO process for FACTS optimization includes:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Initialization}: Define a graph where nodes represent FACTS configurations (e.g., bus locations) and edges represent transitions.
    \item \textbf{Pheromone Update}: Update pheromone levels on edges based on solution quality:

    \[ \tau_{ij} \leftarrow (1 - \rho) \cdot \tau_{ij} + \sum_{\text{ants}} \Delta \tau_{ij} \]

    where \( \tau_{ij} \) is the pheromone level, \( \rho \) is the evaporation rate, and \( \Delta \tau_{ij} \) is the pheromone deposit based on the objective function.
    \item \textbf{Path Selection}: Ants select paths probabilistically based on pheromone levels and heuristic information:

    \[ p_{ij} = \frac{\tau_{ij}^\alpha \cdot \eta_{ij}^\beta}{\sum_{k} \tau_{ik}^\alpha \cdot \eta_{ik}^\beta} \]

    where \( \eta_{ij} \) is the heuristic value (e.g., inverse of cost), and \( \alpha, \beta \) are weighting parameters.
    \item \textbf{Iteration}: Repeat until convergence.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Application to FACTS}
ACO is suitable for optimizing discrete FACTS placement problems, such as selecting optimal bus locations for multiple SVCs in a large system.

\subsection{Advantages and Limitations}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Advantages}: Effective for discrete optimization; good for combinatorial problems; robust to local optima.
    \item \textbf{Limitations}: Computationally intensive; requires careful pheromone management; less effective for continuous variables.
\end{itemize}

\section{LINEAR PROGRAMMING (LP)}
Linear Programming is a deterministic optimization technique that solves problems with linear objective functions and constraints.

\subsection{Formulation}
The LP problem for FACTS optimization can be formulated as:

\[ \text{Minimize: } Z = c^T x \]
\[ \text{Subject to: } Ax \leq b, x \geq 0 \]

where \( x \) represents FACTS device parameters (e.g., reactive power injection), \( c \) is the cost vector, \( A \) is the constraint matrix, and \( b \) is the constraint vector.

\subsection{Application to FACTS}
LP is suitable for simplified power system models, such as optimizing reactive power injection for voltage stability in small networks with linearized power flow equations.

\subsection{Advantages and Limitations}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Advantages}: Simple and efficient; guarantees global optimality for linear problems; fast computation.
    \item \textbf{Limitations}: Limited to linear models; cannot handle nonlinearities or discrete variables; less applicable to complex power systems.
\end{itemize}

\section{HYBRID OPTIMIZATION APPROACHES}
To address the limitations of individual algorithms, hybrid optimization approaches combine the strengths of multiple techniques. For example:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{PSO-GA Hybrid}: Combines PSO’s fast convergence with GA’s global search capability to optimize FACTS placement and sizing.
    \item \textbf{DE-ACO Hybrid}: Integrates DE’s robustness with ACO’s effectiveness for discrete problems, suitable for multi-FACTS placement.
    \item \textbf{PSO-MPC Hybrid}: Combines PSO with model predictive control for real-time FACTS optimization.
\end{itemize}

Hybrid approaches can improve convergence speed, solution quality, and robustness, but they increase computational complexity and require careful design.

Optimization techniques are essential for effective FACTS implementation, enabling engineers to achieve optimal system performance while addressing multiple objectives and constraints. GA, PSO, DE, and ACO are well-suited for complex, nonlinear, and multi-objective problems, while LP offers simplicity for linearized models. Hybrid approaches provide a promising avenue for further improvements. The next chapter applies these techniques to practical case studies to evaluate their effectiveness in real-world power systems.

\section{METHODOLOGY}
The implementation methodology involves a systematic approach to FACTS deployment, integrating power system modeling, optimization, simulation, and validation. The key steps are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Power System Modeling}: Perform power flow analysis using the Newton-Raphson method to model the test systems, incorporating bus data, line data, and load profiles.
    \item \textbf{Objective Function Definition}: Define a multi-objective function to balance power losses, voltage deviations, stability margins, and costs:

    \[ f = w_1 \cdot \sum_{i=1}^{N_l} P_{loss,i} + w_2 \cdot \sum_{j=1}^{N_b} |V_j - 1.0| + w_3 \cdot C_{FACTS} + w_4 \cdot (1 - \lambda_{min}) \]

    where \( \lambda_{min} \) is the minimum eigenvalue of the system Jacobian, representing stability.
    \item \textbf{Optimization}: Apply PSO, GA, DE, or hybrid algorithms to determine optimal FACTS configurations, including device type, location, size, and control settings.
    \item \textbf{Simulation}: Use software tools (e.g., MATLAB with MATPOWER, PSSE, DIgSILENT) to simulate FACTS performance under various operating conditions, including normal operation, contingencies, and renewable integration scenarios.
    \item \textbf{Validation}: Compare results with baseline scenarios (without FACTS) to quantify improvements in power losses, voltage stability, transient stability, and power transfer capability.
    \item \textbf{Sensitivity Analysis}: Evaluate the robustness of solutions under parameter variations, such as load changes or line outages.
\end{enumerate}

\section{CHALLENGES IN IMPLEMENTATION}
The implementation of FACTS devices faces several technical, economic, and operational challenges:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{High Capital Costs}: FACTS devices, particularly UPFCs and IPFCs, require significant investment, ranging from \$5–15 million per installation, depending on power rating and complexity.
    \item \textbf{Computational Complexity}: Optimization algorithms like GA, PSO, DE, and ACO are computationally intensive, requiring high-performance computing resources and significant processing time for large systems.
    \item \textbf{Real-Time Control Requirements}: Dynamic control of FACTS devices demands advanced communication infrastructure (e.g., IEC 61850 protocols) and fast-response control systems to handle transient events and renewable variability.
    \item \textbf{Integration with Renewable Energy**: The intermittent nature of wind and solar power introduces variability, complicating FACTS control and requiring adaptive strategies.
    \item \textbf{Harmonic Distortion and Thermal Constraints}: Power electronic components can introduce harmonics, necessitating filters, while high power ratings lead to thermal stress, limiting device lifespan.
    \item \textbf{Coordination with Existing Infrastructure}: Integrating FACTS devices with legacy systems requires careful planning to avoid conflicts with existing control mechanisms.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Proposed Solutions}
To address these challenges, the following solutions are proposed:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Cost Reduction}: Develop modular FACTS designs and leverage economies of scale through standardized components and mass production.
    \item \textbf{Computational Efficiency}: Use parallel computing, cloud-based optimization platforms, and hybrid algorithms to reduce computation time and improve scalability.
    \item \textbf{Real-Time Control}: Implement advanced communication protocols (e.g., 5G, IEC 61850) and AI-based control systems (e.g., neural networks, reinforcement learning) for dynamic operation.
    \item \textbf{Renewable Integration}: Develop adaptive FACTS controllers that use predictive models to mitigate renewable variability, supported by energy storage systems.
    \item \textbf{Harmonic Mitigation}: Incorporate active filters and advanced converter designs to reduce harmonic distortion.
    \item \textbf{Thermal Management}: Use advanced cooling systems and predictive maintenance to extend device lifespan.
    \item \textbf{Coordination Strategies}: Implement wide-area monitoring systems (e.g., PMUs) and coordinated control frameworks to integrate FACTS with existing infrastructure.
\end{itemize}

\section{FUTURE ENHANCEMENTS}
The results and analyses from the case studies involving the IEEE 14-bus, IEEE 30-bus, and IEEE 57-bus test systems underscore the transformative potential of Flexible AC Transmission Systems (FACTS) devices in enhancing power system performance. However, several challenges, including high capital costs, computational complexity, and the need for robust real-time control, highlight the necessity for continued research and innovation. This section outlines a comprehensive set of future enhancements to advance FACTS technology, addressing technical, economic, and operational barriers while aligning with emerging trends in power system management, such as renewable energy integration, smart grid development, and cybersecurity. These enhancements are categorized into five key areas: advanced optimization techniques, real-time control systems, renewable energy integration, cost reduction strategies, and smart grid and cybersecurity integration.

\subsection{Advanced Optimization Techniques}
The optimization techniques evaluated in this project—Genetic Algorithm (GA), Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO), Differential Evolution (DE), Ant Colony Optimization (ACO), and Linear Programming (LP)—demonstrated significant potential for FACTS implementation. However, their computational complexity and sensitivity to parameter settings limit their scalability for large-scale, real-time applications. Future research should focus on developing hybrid and adaptive optimization algorithms to combine the strengths of individual techniques while mitigating their weaknesses.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Hybrid Algorithms}: Hybrid approaches, such as PSO-GA or DE-ACO, can leverage PSO’s fast convergence and GA’s global search capability to optimize FACTS placement and sizing. For example, a PSO-GA hybrid could initialize solutions using PSO’s swarm intelligence and refine them through GA’s crossover and mutation, achieving better solution quality with reduced computational time. Preliminary studies suggest that hybrid algorithms can reduce convergence time by 20–30\% compared to standalone methods.
    \item \textbf{Adaptive Optimization}: Adaptive algorithms that dynamically adjust parameters (e.g., inertia weight in PSO, mutation rate in GA) based on system conditions can improve robustness and scalability. For instance, an adaptive PSO could increase the cognitive parameter during early iterations to explore the solution space and reduce it later to exploit local optima, enhancing performance in dynamic power systems.
    \item \textbf{Multi-Objective Optimization Frameworks}: Advanced frameworks, such as Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II (NSGA-II) or Multi-Objective Particle Swarm Optimization (MOPSO), can simultaneously optimize conflicting objectives (e.g., power losses, voltage stability, costs, and transient stability). These frameworks generate a Pareto front of solutions, allowing engineers to select trade-offs based on specific priorities.
    \item \textbf{Parallel and Distributed Computing}: Leveraging parallel computing platforms, such as GPU-based systems or cloud computing, can significantly reduce the computational burden of optimization algorithms. For example, parallelizing PSO’s particle evaluations across multiple processors can reduce computation time by up to 50\% for large systems like the IEEE 57-bus network.
\end{itemize}

Future research should also explore emerging optimization techniques, such as Firefly Algorithm (FA), Artificial Bee Colony (ABC), and Grey Wolf Optimizer (GWO), which have shown promise in power system applications. These algorithms could offer improved convergence and solution quality for complex FACTS optimization problems, particularly in systems with high renewable penetration.

\subsection{Real-Time Control Systems}
The dynamic nature of modern power systems, characterized by rapid load variations, renewable energy fluctuations, and transient disturbances, necessitates real-time control systems for FACTS devices. Current control strategies, such as PID and vector control, are effective but lack the adaptability required for highly variable conditions. Future enhancements should focus on developing advanced, intelligent control systems to enable dynamic and predictive operation.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Artificial Intelligence (AI)-Based Control}: AI techniques, such as deep reinforcement learning (DRL) and neural networks, can enable adaptive FACTS control by learning optimal control policies from historical and real-time data. For example, a DRL-based controller could optimize UPFC settings to minimize power losses and stabilize voltages during renewable power fluctuations, achieving response times below 5 ms.
    \item \textbf{Model Predictive Control (MPC)}: MPC uses predictive models to optimize FACTS operation over a finite time horizon, accounting for system constraints and future states. An MPC-based STATCOM controller could predict voltage deviations based on load forecasts and adjust reactive power output proactively, improving stability by 10–15\% compared to traditional methods.
    \item \textbf{Wide-Area Monitoring and Control}: Integrating FACTS devices with wide-area monitoring systems (WAMS), such as Phasor Measurement Units (PMUs), can enhance real-time control by providing synchronized, high-resolution data on system states. A WAMS-based TCSC controller could dampen inter-area oscillations in real time, reducing damping times by 20–30\%.
    \item \textbf{Decentralized and Multi-Agent Control}: Multi-agent systems, where FACTS devices operate as autonomous agents coordinating through communication networks, can improve scalability and fault tolerance. For instance, a decentralized UPFC network could optimize power flow across multiple lines, adapting to local and global system conditions without centralized control.
\end{itemize}

These advancements require robust communication infrastructure, such as 5G networks and IEC 61850 protocols, to ensure low-latency, reliable data exchange. Future research should also address cybersecurity risks associated with real-time control, as discussed later.

\subsection{Renewable Energy Integration}
The increasing penetration of renewable energy sources, such as wind and solar power, introduces variability and uncertainty that challenge power system stability and operation. FACTS devices play a critical role in mitigating these challenges, but their control strategies must be optimized for renewable-heavy grids. Future enhancements should focus on developing specialized FACTS controllers and coordination strategies to support high renewable penetration.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Predictive Control for Renewable Variability}: Predictive control algorithms that use weather forecasts and machine learning to anticipate renewable power fluctuations can optimize FACTS operation. For example, a predictive STATCOM controller could pre-adjust reactive power output to mitigate voltage fluctuations caused by sudden wind power drops, reducing fluctuations by 15–20\%.
    \item \textbf{Coordination with Energy Storage Systems}: Integrating FACTS devices with energy storage systems (ESS), such as battery energy storage systems (BESS), can enhance renewable integration by smoothing power output and providing backup reactive power. A coordinated STATCOM-BESS system could maintain voltage stability during renewable outages, improving reliability by 25\%.
    \item \textbf{Hybrid Renewable-FACTS Systems}: Developing hybrid systems that combine FACTS devices with renewable energy converters (e.g., wind turbine inverters) can optimize power flow and stability. For instance, a hybrid UPFC-wind system could use the wind converter’s reactive power capability to supplement UPFC control, reducing device sizing requirements by 10–15\%.
    \item \textbf{Microgrid Integration}: FACTS devices can enhance the stability and efficiency of microgrids with high renewable penetration. A TCSC deployed in a microgrid could optimize power exchange with the main grid, reducing losses by 10\% and improving voltage stability by 12\%.
\end{itemize}

Future research should also explore the role of FACTS devices in supporting grid-forming inverters, which are critical for renewable-dominated grids transitioning to inverter-based operation.

\subsection{Cost Reduction Strategies}
The high capital costs of FACTS devices, particularly UPFCs and IPFCs, remain a significant barrier to widespread adoption. Future enhancements should focus on developing cost-effective designs and implementation strategies to improve accessibility and economic viability.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Modular FACTS Designs}: Modular designs that allow scalable, plug-and-play installation can reduce manufacturing and installation costs. For example, a modular STATCOM with standardized 50 MVAR units could be scaled to meet system requirements, reducing costs by 20–30\% compared to custom designs.
    \item \textbf{Standardized Components}: Using standardized power electronic components, such as IGBTs and capacitors, can leverage economies of scale and reduce costs. Industry-wide standardization could lower UPFC costs by 15–25\% over the next decade.
    \item \textbf{Advanced Manufacturing Techniques}: Adopting advanced manufacturing techniques, such as 3D printing for converter housings or automated assembly for control circuits, can reduce production costs and improve reliability.
    \item \textbf{Cost-Benefit Optimization}: Developing optimization frameworks that explicitly minimize installation and operational costs while maximizing system benefits can improve economic viability. For instance, a cost-optimized PSO algorithm could select smaller FACTS devices with equivalent performance, reducing costs by 10–20\%.
\end{itemize}

These strategies should be complemented by policy incentives, such as subsidies or tax breaks, to encourage FACTS adoption in developing regions and renewable-heavy grids.

\subsection{Smart Grid and Cybersecurity Integration}
The transition to smart grids, characterized by advanced metering, distributed energy resources, and real-time monitoring, presents both opportunities and challenges for FACTS implementation. Future enhancements should focus on integrating FACTS devices with smart grid technologies while addressing cybersecurity risks.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Smart Grid Integration}: FACTS devices can enhance smart grid operation by optimizing power flow, supporting demand response, and stabilizing distributed energy resources. For example, a UPFC integrated with a smart grid could prioritize power flow to critical loads during peak demand, improving efficiency by 10–15\%.
    \item \textbf{Demand Response Coordination}: Coordinating FACTS devices with demand response programs can reduce peak loads and improve stability. A STATCOM coordinated with demand response could reduce voltage deviations by 8–12\% during peak periods.
    \item \textbf{Cybersecurity Frameworks}: The reliance on communication networks for real-time FACTS control introduces cybersecurity risks, such as data tampering or denial-of-service attacks. Developing robust cybersecurity frameworks, including encryption, intrusion detection, and secure protocols, is critical. For instance, implementing blockchain-based authentication for FACTS control signals could reduce cyber risks by 30–40\%.
    \item \textbf{Resilient Control Systems}: Designing resilient control systems that operate effectively under cyber-attacks or communication failures can enhance reliability. A fault-tolerant UPFC controller could maintain stable operation during a 50\% communication loss, ensuring system stability.
\end{itemize}

Future research should also explore the integration of FACTS devices with Internet of Things (IoT) platforms for real-time monitoring and predictive maintenance, reducing operational costs by 10–15\%.

\section{DISCUSSION OF IMPLEMENTATION CHALLENGES}
The case studies highlighted several implementation challenges that must be addressed to realize the full potential of FACTS devices. These challenges, along with their implications and proposed solutions, are discussed below to provide a comprehensive understanding of the barriers to FACTS adoption and strategies for overcoming them.

\subsection{Economic Barriers}
The high capital costs of FACTS devices, ranging from \$5–15 million per installation, pose a significant barrier, particularly for utilities in developing regions. These costs are driven by the complexity of power electronic components, custom engineering requirements, and installation expenses. The cost-benefit analysis in Section 5.3 demonstrated payback periods of 6–7 years, which may deter investment in budget-constrained utilities.

\textbf{Solution}: In addition to modular designs and standardized components, public-private partnerships and international funding mechanisms, such as World Bank grants, can offset initial costs. Utilities can also adopt phased implementation strategies, starting with smaller FACTS devices (e.g., SVCs) and scaling to UPFCs as benefits are realized.

\subsection{Technical Complexity}
The nonlinear and dynamic nature of power systems complicates FACTS control and optimization. The computational complexity of algorithms like GA and ACO, which require thousands of iterations for large systems, limits their applicability in real-time scenarios. Additionally, the integration of FACTS devices with legacy infrastructure requires careful coordination to avoid conflicts with existing control systems.

\textbf{Solution}: The proposed hybrid algorithms and parallel computing platforms can reduce computational time, while decentralized control systems can simplify integration with legacy infrastructure. Developing standardized interfaces for FACTS devices, similar to plug-and-play standards in consumer electronics, can further streamline deployment.

\subsection{Operational Challenges}
Real-time control of FACTS devices demands advanced communication infrastructure and fast-response systems, which are often lacking in existing grids. The variability introduced by renewable energy sources further complicates control, requiring adaptive strategies to handle sudden power fluctuations. Harmonic distortion and thermal constraints also pose operational risks, potentially reducing device lifespan.

\textbf{Solution}: The integration of 5G networks and IEC 61850 protocols can ensure low-latency communication, while AI-based control systems can adapt to renewable variability. Active filters and advanced cooling systems can mitigate harmonics and thermal stress, extending device lifespan by 5–10 years.

\subsection{Cybersecurity Risks}
The reliance on communication networks for FACTS control introduces cybersecurity risks, including data tampering, unauthorized access, and denial-of-service attacks. These risks are particularly critical in smart grids, where interconnected devices increase the attack surface.

\textbf{Solution}: Implementing robust cybersecurity frameworks, such as end-to-end encryption, intrusion detection systems, and blockchain-based authentication, can mitigate risks. Regular security audits and resilience testing can further ensure system integrity.

The findings of this project highlight several promising directions for future research to further enhance FACTS implementation and address emerging challenges in power system management. The following areas are recommended for continued investigation:

These controllers should aim to reduce renewable curtailment by 50\% and voltage fluctuations by 20–25\%.
    \item \textbf{Cost-Effective and Scalable Designs}: Develop modular, standardized FACTS designs to reduce capital costs by 20–30\% and improve scalability for small and medium utilities. Research should also explore low-cost alternatives, such as hybrid FACTS-renewable systems, to enhance accessibility in developing regions.
    \item \textbf{Cybersecurity and Resilience}: Establish robust cybersecurity frameworks for FACTS control, including blockchain-based authentication, intrusion detection, and resilient control algorithms. Future studies should aim to reduce cyber risks by 30–40\% and ensure stable operation during communication failures.
    \item \textbf{Multi-Objective and Large-Scale Optimization}: Advance multi-objective optimization frameworks, such as NSGA-II and MOPSO, to handle large-scale systems with thousands of buses. These frameworks should incorporate parallel computing and cloud-based platforms to reduce computation time by 50\% and improve solution quality by 10–15\%.
    \item \textbf{Harmonic and Thermal Management}: Develop advanced harmonic filters and thermal management systems to mitigate distortion and extend FACTS device lifespan by 5–10 years. Research should also explore predictive maintenance using machine learning to reduce downtime by 20\%.
    \item \textbf Policy and Regulatory Support}: Collaborate with policymakers to develop incentives, such as subsidies and tax breaks, to encourage FACTS adoption. Future studies should assess the socio-economic impact of FACTS deployment, particularly in rural and renewable-heavy regions.
\end{itemize}

These research directions aim to build on the findings of this project, addressing both immediate and long-term challenges in FACTS implementation. By pursuing these areas, researchers can contribute to the development of more reliable, efficient, and sustainable power systems, supporting the global transition to clean energy.

\chapter{MATHEMATICAL DERIVATIONS}
\section{POWER FLOW EQUATIONS}
The power flow equations for a power system with FACTS devices are derived as follows:

For a bus \( i \) connected to bus \( j \), the active and reactive power injections are:

\[ P_i = V_i \sum_{j=1}^N V_j (G_{ij} \cos \theta_{ij} + B_{ij} \sin \theta_{ij}) \]
\[ Q_i = V_i \sum_{j=1}^N V_j (G_{ij} \sin \theta_{ij} - B_{ij} \cos \theta_{ij}) \]

where \( V_i, V_j \) are voltage magnitudes, \( \theta_{ij} = \theta_i - \theta_j \) is the phase angle difference, and \( G_{ij}, B_{ij} \) are the conductance and susceptance of the admittance matrix, modified by FACTS devices (e.g., TCSC reactance, UPFC voltage injection).

\section{OPTIMIZATION FORMULATIONS}
The multi-objective optimization problem for FACTS placement is formulated as:

\[ \text{Minimize: } f(x) = w_1 \cdot P_{loss}(x) + w_2 \cdot V_{dev}(x) + w_3 \cdot C_{FACTS}(x) + w_4 \cdot (1 - \lambda_{min}(x)) \]

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \( P_{loss} = \sum_{i=1}^{N_l} G_{ij} (V_i^2 + V_j^2 - 2 V_i V_j \cos \theta_{ij}) \)
    \item \( V_{dev} = \sum_{i=1}^{N_b} |V_i - 1.0| \)
    \item \( C_{FACTS} \): Cost function based on device type and size
    \item \( \lambda_{min} \): Minimum eigenvalue of the Jacobian matrix
\end{itemize}

Constraints include voltage limits (0.95–1.05 p.u.), line flow limits, and device ratings.

# Sample PSO code for FACTS placement
import numpy as np

def objective_function(x):
    # Calculate power loss and voltage deviation
    power_loss = compute_power_loss(x)  # Placeholder for power flow calculation
    voltage_deviation = compute_voltage_deviation(x)
    return 0.5 * power_loss + 0.5 * voltage_deviation

def pso_optimize(n_particles, iterations, w=0.8, c1=2.0, c2=2.0):
    particles = np.random.rand(n_particles, 2)  # FACTS location and size
    velocities = np.zeros((n_particles, 2))
    pbest = particles.copy()
    pbest_scores = np.array([objective_function(p) for p in pbest])
    gbest = particles[np.argmin(pbest_scores)]
    gbest_score = np.min(pbest_scores)
    
    for _ in range(iterations):
        for i in range(n_particles):
            r1, r2 = np.random.rand(2)
            velocities[i] = (w * velocities[i] +
                             c1 * r1 * (pbest[i] - particles[i]) +
                             c2 * r2 * (gbest - particles[i]))
            particles[i] = particles[i] + velocities[i]
            particles[i] = np.clip(particles[i], 0, 1)  # Enforce bounds
            score = objective_function(particles[i])
            if score < pbest_scores[i]:
                pbest[i] = particles[i]
                pbest_scores[i] = score
            if score < gbest_score:
                gbest = particles[i]
                gbest_score = score
    
    return gbest, gbest_score

# Run optimization
optimal_solution, optimal_score = pso_optimize(50, 100)
print(f"Optimal Solution: {optimal_solution}, Score: {optimal_score}")
